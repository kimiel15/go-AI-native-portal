01
Part 1
Identify the problem
Arrive at the build phase with a specific, data-backed problem your squad lead believes in.
Look at the data

Your squad lead will share the data with you. When you sit with it, look for two types of signals. Write down the one or two numbers that surprise you most — those are your starting points.

Containment signals
  • Which contact reasons have the highest volume?
  • Which ones keep escalating from the chatbot to a human?
  • Which ones do customers almost always fail to resolve on their own?
  • Where is your squad's biggest containment gap?
Revenue signals
  • Where is the gap between your best and average engineer on upsell or save rate?
  • Which interaction types consistently miss the revenue moment?
  • Where are customers leaving without being retained or converted?
Locate your problem in the landscape

You saw two landscapes at the kickoff. Now use them to find where your data sits.

For each contact reason, ask:

At the prevention level
"Could a customer have found the answer themselves — if the right content or tool existed?"
At the chatbot level
"Could the chatbot handle this with better prompts and knowledge?"
At the self-service level
"Could a customer have resolved this themselves — if a clear tool or guided flow existed?"
Examples by channel

Chat contacts that customers could have resolved themselves:

  • Customer asking if their subscription is still active
  • Customer asking when their renewal date is
  • Customer asking how to turn off auto-renewal
  • Customer asking what is included in their current plan
  • Customer asking how to install on a new device
  • Customer asking why they were charged a certain amount

Phone calls that customers could have handled without calling:

  • Call just to confirm their renewal date
  • Call asking how to remove a device from their account
  • Call asking if their antivirus is still running
  • Call asking how to update their billing information
  • Call asking if a specific website or file is safe
  • Call asking how to transfer their license to a new device

Email contacts that customers could have resolved without sending:

  • Email asking whether a refund request qualifies
  • Email asking for a copy of their invoice or receipt
  • Email asking how to cancel auto-renewal before it charges
  • Email asking what the renewal price will be
  • Email asking how to transfer their license to a new device
  • Email asking what their current plan covers
These are starting points — your squad data will surface the real opportunity.
Size it

Contact volume on that reason × expected containment improvement = weekly contacts deflected.

Example: 200 contacts per week × 50% containment = 100 contacts deflected per week.

For each interaction type, ask:

Exploring Purchase
"Do engineers discover the need first — or jump straight to a product?"
Upgrade Opportunity
"Is renewal or pricing turned into a value conversation — or treated as a transaction?"
Support Recovery
"Do engineers leave customers retained and reassured — or just resolved?"
Protection Gap
"Is the trust moment used to guide toward better protection — or just fix the issue?"
Size it

Interactions per week × conservative improvement in offer or save rate × average revenue value per outcome.

Example: 200 cancellations × +10pp save rate = 20 additional saves × $80 = $1,600 per week.
Talk to your squad lead

This is the most important step. Do not skip it.

Your squad lead knows things the data doesn't show — which problems are more painful than their volume suggests, where revenue moments are being missed, and what a realistic improvement looks like.

Start here
"I looked at the data. I think the biggest opportunity for us is [problem]. The number I'm seeing is [X]. Does this match what you see on the floor?"

Then listen. They will confirm, sharpen, or redirect. The output of that conversation is a problem statement both of you believe in.

First
Deployment authorization
Your squad lead needs to believe in the problem enough to authorize deployment. If they're not convinced, you'll hit friction at the worst moment.
Second
Specificity
Their domain knowledge turns a vague problem into a specific one — and specificity is what makes a tool buildable in two weeks.
Write the problem statement

One paragraph. Five things in it. If you can write it and your squad lead signs off — you're ready for Part 2.

Squad
Your squad name
Target
40% AI Containment
20% Revenue Growth
What's broken, for whom, and when
One paragraph describing exactly what's happening today.
The number that proves it
Volume, rate, or gap — from the data your squad lead shared.
Connection to the target
"If we solve this, we expect to move [metric] by [amount], contributing to the [containment / revenue] target because [reason]."
What success looks like
The specific measurable outcome during the production run.
Why it's solvable in the build window
One sentence — no system integrations needed, buildable in two weeks.
Baseline locked
The current number, the reference period, confirmed by squad lead. See Part 2.

02
Part 2
Lock the baseline
Record the current number before anything is built.

The baseline is the number that proves something changed. Without it, your production run data has no before-and-after — it's just a number. Lock it before you build. Not after. Not after you deploy. Now.

Use the last four weeks as your reference window. Your squad lead confirms the number is accurate.

Primary metric
Contact volume on the targeted reason
How many contacts per week does your squad receive on this specific reason? Average over the last four weeks.
Containment rate
Current rate on this reason
What percentage of contacts on this reason are currently resolved without an engineer? Could be zero.
Channel context
How does this contact arrive?
Chat, phones, or email — and whether it currently escalates from the chatbot or arrives directly.
Reference period
Last four weeks
Use the same period consistently. Don't cherry-pick a low week as your baseline.
Primary metric
Current rate on the targeted revenue moment
Save rate, conversion rate, or upsell offer rate — whichever your tool targets. Average over the last four weeks.
Best vs average gap
Engineer performance spread
What does your best engineer achieve? What does the squad average? The gap is your opportunity.
Interaction volume
Weekly count for this interaction type
How many cancellation chats, upsell moments, or renewal conversations does your squad handle per week?
Revenue value
Average value per positive outcome
Average subscription value or upsell revenue per save or conversion. Your squad lead helps confirm this.
Write it down and keep it visible

Once locked, this number does not change. It's the reference point for everything that follows. The whole team should know what it is before the build starts.

03
Part 3
Build the tool
Build something that addresses the specific problem — ready to deploy before the production run starts.

May enablement covered the mechanics. This part frames the intent. Three principles matter most during the build.

1
Build for the problem, not for the demo
The tool should do one thing well — address the specific gap you identified in Part 1. Not the most impressive thing you can build with Claude. The most useful thing for your problem.
2
Every team member commits
Building is a team activity. Each member should have at least one meaningful contribution — prompts, code, testing, validation, or documentation. All of it counts.
3
Test before you deploy
Run the tool on sample or historical cases before it goes live. Edge cases — customers who don't fit the pattern, incomplete inputs, unexpected contact types — should be handled gracefully. A tool that breaks during the production run hurts both the result and the submission.
Squad lead authorization

Before the tool goes live, the squad lead reviews and authorizes the production run. They're not just approving software — they're attesting that the tool is ready to run on real customer interactions and that the measurement methodology is sound.

04
Part 4
Measure the impact
One week. The team captures daily. The squad lead validates at the end.

Numbers and analysis are the evidence. Numbers without analysis are just data. Analysis makes them evidence.

Capture daily

One week goes fast. Log your numbers every day — not at the end of the week from memory. Daily capture gives you a real picture of how the tool performed.

Log each day
Total contacts received on the targeted reason
Contacts deflected — resolved without reaching an engineer through your tool
Tool sessions or completions (if portal-deployed)
Any issues, edge cases, or tool failures
Total at end of week
Total contacts on targeted reason across the week
Total deflected contacts across the week
Containment rate: deflected ÷ total on this reason × 100
Comparison to baseline
What counts as deflected

A contact is deflected when a customer resolves their issue through your tool without reaching an engineer. Portal session completed = deflected. Chatbot handled end-to-end = deflected. Customer still escalates = not deflected.

Log each day
Total targeted interactions handled
Interactions where the tool was used
Outcomes achieved — saves, conversions, or upsells
Whether the tool recommendation was used, modified, or not used
Total at end of week
Total targeted interactions across the week
Total positive outcomes
Rate: positive outcomes ÷ total targeted interactions × 100
Comparison to baseline rate
What counts as tool-influenced

An outcome is tool-influenced when the engineer invoked the tool and it shaped their approach. The engineer doesn't have to follow the recommendation exactly — but it should have contributed to the result.

Analyse at the end of the week
1
Calculate the containment rate
How many contacts were deflected during the production run week?
Containment rate = contacts deflected ÷ total contacts on reason × 100
2
Calculate the improvement
Production run rate versus baseline rate. This is your headline number.
Improvement = production run rate − baseline rate
3
Calculate the weekly capacity freed
Contacts deflected multiplied by average handle time on this reason.
Capacity freed = deflected contacts × avg handle time (minutes)
4
Project the annual impact
Weekly deflection multiplied by 52. Label clearly as a projection based on the measured week.
Annual projection = weekly deflected contacts × 52
5
Explain the trend
Did the containment rate improve as you refined the tool? Were certain contact types easier to deflect? What would v1.1 address?
1
Calculate the production run rate
Save rate, conversion rate, or upsell offer rate during the production run week.
Rate = positive outcomes ÷ total targeted interactions × 100
2
Calculate the improvement
Production run rate versus baseline rate. This is your headline number.
Improvement = production run rate − baseline rate
3
Calculate the weekly revenue impact
Additional positive outcomes per week multiplied by average revenue value per outcome.
Weekly revenue impact = additional outcomes × avg revenue value
4
Project the annual impact
Weekly revenue impact multiplied by 52. Label as a projection based on the measured week.
Annual projection = weekly revenue impact × 52
5
Explain what drove the change
Did engineers use the tool consistently? Did recommendation quality improve over the week? Which interaction types responded best?
Be honest about the numbers
✓
If the result is smaller than expected — report it accurately. A modest result with clean evidence is stronger than an inflated result with weak methodology.
✓
If the tool didn't work on certain interaction types — say so in the analysis. Explain what you would improve next. That's a better story than pretending it worked perfectly.
✓
If the baseline week was atypically high or low — note it and explain why.
✓
Projections are projections — always label annualised estimates as projections based on the measured week. Never present them as confirmed outcomes.
05
Part 5
Submit the evidence
Everything goes in your GitHub team folder when the production run ends.

The submission is the tool and the evidence together — uploaded to your team folder in the ai-tech-challenge GitHub repository. Both are required. A tool without evidence does not compete.

🛠️
The tool
Built with C4E skills, Claude Code, or Cowork. Uploaded to your team folder in GitHub. Your README.md must cover all six sections: Problem Statement (data/evidence the problem is real) · Solution (how the tool solves it) · Production Evidence & Results (data from the pilot period) · Contribution (what each member did) · Claude Usage (how Claude was used to build it) · Usage Instructions (simple step-by-step guide to run it).
📊
The baseline
The metric, the reference period, how it was calculated. One paragraph. Enough for a judge to verify.
📋
The daily log
Seven days of data. Numbers per day, not just the weekly total. Shows the trend and makes the result auditable.
📈
The improvement
Production run rate or volume compared to the baseline. The headline number. Simple, clean, honest.
🧮
The impact calculation
Weekly impact from the improvement. Annual projection labelled as a projection. Methodology shown step by step.
📝
The analysis
What happened, why it happened, what the trend looked like, and what you would improve next.
Squad lead validation — before submission
  • Baseline is accurate — reflects the squad's actual pre-tool performance
  • Production run was real — tool ran on live interactions or live portal traffic
  • Daily log is complete — no days missing, numbers are as reported
  • Impact calculation is correct — methodology is sound, projections are labelled
  • Analysis is honest — results reported accurately including any limitations
  • Every team member contributed meaningfully
!
For reference
Common traps

Things that derail builds before they start — or submissions before they land.

⚠️
The solution in search of a problem
Starting with the technology — "I want to build a chatbot" — instead of the problem produces tools nobody uses.
Fix: Let the data lead. The technology is the last decision, not the first.
⚠️
The unmeasurable problem
If you can't measure the current state and the improvement, you can't prove your tool made a difference.
Fix: Every problem needs a before-and-after metric. Name it before you build.
⚠️
The too-big problem
Problems that span multiple contact reasons or channels produce unfocused tools that don't do anything particularly well.
Fix: One contact reason, one channel, one gap. Narrower is stronger.
⚠️
The orphaned build
No squad lead buy-in means no deployment authorization, no evidence, and no submission.
Fix: The squad lead conversation in Part 1 is not optional.
⚠️
The missing baseline
Trying to establish the baseline after the tool has already run makes the before-and-after unprovable.
Fix: Lock it in Part 2. Before the build. Not after.
⚠️
The projection without evidence
Estimates without a measured baseline won't hold up. Judges verify the numbers and the methodology behind them.
Fix: Know your baseline before you build. Show your working in the impact calculation.

Build something that moves a number.

The best submissions won't be the most technically impressive. They'll be the ones that identified a real problem, built something specific to it, measured it honestly, and told the story clearly. That's the guide.
